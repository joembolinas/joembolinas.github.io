The **Gibbs' Reflective Cycle** is a structured, six-stage model widely adopted for exhaustive academic and experiential reflection.

The six stages are:

1. **Description:** The individual details the experience or event without judgment, including what happened, who was involved, their roles, the tools used, and the overall outcome. The goal is to provide a clear, objective account.
2. **Feelings:** The individual reflects on and expresses the thoughts and emotions experienced before, during, and after the event. This stage focuses on understanding the personal and affective impact of the experience, such as engagement and motivation.
3. **Evaluation:** The individual judges the experience by assessing what went well and what did not, identifying both successes and challenges. This stage helps determine the strengths and weaknesses of the experience.
4. **Analysis:** The individual explores *why* things happened the way they did, delving deeper into causes and effects, considering different perspectives, and explicitly linking the experience to theoretical concepts or previous knowledge.
5. **Conclusion:** The individual summarizes the key lessons learned, what could have been done differently, and how the situation might have been improved.
6. **Action Plan:** The individual creates a plan for applying the learning to future similar situations or projects, setting specific goals to improve future performance.

For rigorous technical reflection, these six stages ensure systematic coverage of the project lifecycle, progressing logically from objective description to prescriptive planning.

The sources define **capstone documentation** (often referred to as a "reflection essay") as a critical, high-level deliverable that serves a pivotal **role** in synthesizing a student's entire educational journey and meeting essential professional  **requirements** .

### Role of Capstone Documentation

The capstone documentation's primary role is to serve as  **the definitive document of the student's educational journey** , moving beyond personal description to document professional transformation.

1. **Synthesizing Knowledge:** It is explicitly identified as the  **capstone documentation for synthesizing theoretical learning with practical, often complex, technical experience** . This process is defined as the successful conversion of raw technical experience into verifiable theoretical understanding.
2. **Measure of Professional Maturity:** In computer science, this document is a **critical assessment of applied knowledge** and a direct  **measure of professional maturity** . It serves as a vehicle for demonstrating measured progress toward  **career readiness and success within the major** .
3. **Holistic Assessment:** The documentation must articulate how learning advanced specific professional competencies, encompassing  **technical skills (Cognitive), team dynamics (Social), and personal growth (Affective)** . It is a holistic assessment that captures the student's overall development, including their confidence, motivation, and perspective on the field.

### Requirements for Capstone Documentation

To fulfill its role, capstone documentation must adhere to stringent **requirements** related to depth, rigor, structure, and professional standards.

#### 1. Requirement for Analytical Depth (Rigor)

The primary requirement is that the documentation **transcends a personal journal entry** and moves from merely describing events to providing  **sustained critical analysis** .

* **Analytical Rigor:** Achieving rigor requires moving beyond Descriptive Analytics (what happened) to utilizing **Diagnostic Analytics** (explaining *why* it happened by linking outcomes to technical theory) and **Prescriptive Analytics** (defining *what should be done* in the future). This progression is essential for creating an authoritative, data-driven assessment of technical choices.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The document must demonstrate the link between practical challenges and relevant academic theories, such as analyzing system performance using Big O notation or justifying architectural decisions using design patterns.

#### 2. Requirement for Structure and Frameworks

The content must be structured formally using established reflective frameworks to ensure systematic coverage and analytical depth.

* **Systematic Structure:** The essay must adhere to a standard academic structure, typically utilizing the adapted **Gibbs’ Reflective Cycle** as the primary organizational scaffolding. This structure ensures a logical progression through stages like Description, Analysis, Conclusion, and Action Plan.
* **Thesis Statement:** The introduction must define the subject, articulate the scope, and culminate in a  **reflective thesis statement** —an overarching articulation of the core lesson learned or professional transformation achieved.

#### 3. Requirement for Evidence (Artifact Fidelity)

Claims made in the documentation must be substantiated with tangible evidence, or  **artifacts** , to establish credibility.

* **Artifact Curation:** The documentation must select high-value technical deliverables (e.g., code, diagrams, test scripts) that directly support the claims of learning transformation.
* **Artifact Fidelity:** The document must explicitly link the objective artifact (e.g., describing a code snippet's function) to a subjective, analytical interpretation of its significance (e.g., affirming mastery of a principle).

#### 4. Requirement for Ethical Integration

A mandatory requirement in contemporary computing is the **reflection upon the wider societal and ethical impacts** of professional work.

* **Ethical Analysis:** The documentation must address the **developer’s specific role in ethical decision-making** and align technical decisions with ethical frameworks, such as the ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct. The assessment should analyze how programming choices (like conditional logic or feature weighting) might compromise or affirm ethical principles.

#### 5. Requirement for Professional Presentation

The final document must adhere to high standards of professional presentation, especially when incorporating technical elements.

* **Formatting Standards:** Rigorous adherence to academic formatting guidelines (e.g., APA, MLA, IEEE) is required. Specific technical requirements include using a **monospaced typeface** for embedded code, maintaining consistent indentation, and using captions and labels to document code snippets and visual aids. Furthermore, claims must reference internal documentation by citing the **exact location** within the source to maintain citational integrity.

In essence, the capstone documentation is required to function as an **integrated professional portfolio** that uses evidence and critical analysis to validate the student’s readiness to operate as a self-directed, reflective, and ethically responsible professional.
